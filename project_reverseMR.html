Reverse Mendelian randomization Visualization Tool

  • by:Pedrum Mohammadi-Shemirani

A dashboard for visualizing results of a reverse Mendelian randomization analysis that can be found at https://pedrum.shinyapps.io/mr_dataviz/. Options include: detection and filtering of outliers, identification of clusters, and biological pathway overlays.

Background

The idea for this visualization tool came from a personal need. My Master's project involved creating a novel variation of an epidemiological method called "Mendelian randomization".

What is Mendelian randomization?

Essentially, Mendelian randomization works similar to a randomized controlled trial (often used in the context of testing whether a drug causes a disease to be cured). In a randomized controlled trial, individuals are randomly allocated to an experimental/drug vs. placebo group. With a sufficiently large sample size, randomization ensures both groups are equal on average in terms of all factors. Therefore, any differences over the course of a trial must be caused by the one factor we have explicitly altered - allocation of the drug vs. placebo. By the same principle, Mendelian randomization is mother nature's RCT. At birth, we are all randomly allocated different versions of genes (alleles) that correspond to higher/lower levels of certain behavioural traits (e.g. smoking), molecular traits (e.g. cholesterol), etc. For instance, if we find an allele that increases levels of testosterone leads to higher rates of heart disease, then it suggests testosterone causes heart disease. In contrast, by simply comparing groups of individuals with high and low testosterone, you cannot draw the same conclusion of causality. It's possible heart disease causes higher testosterone (reverse causation), or some unmeasured third variable is responsible for both higher testosterone and higher rates of heart disease (confounding). For more information, please read this review paper.

Nevertheless, our variation of Mendelian randomization was called "reverse" Mendelian randomization. In traditional MR, we are often looking at molecular traits that cause disease. These are likely good targets for pharmaceutical companies to halt progression of a disease. However, there is also value in finding molecular traits that are themselves caused BY a disease. These would be good targets to diagnose and identify patients developing a disease.
